Wetland Ecosystems

Wetlands are unique ecosystems where water saturates the soil either permanently or seasonally, creating habitats that support both aquatic and terrestrial life. They include marshes, swamps, bogs, and fens and occur in inland, coastal, and even mountainous regions. Despite covering only about 6% of the Earth's surface, wetlands are among the most productive ecosystems, providing essential services like water purification, flood control, carbon storage, and biodiversity support.

Types of Wetlands

Inland Wetlands

  • Found in floodplains, river basins, lakes, and peatlands.
  • Include freshwater marshes, swamps, and seasonal floodplains.
  • Crucial for groundwater recharge and biodiversity conservation.

Coastal Wetlands

  • Include mangroves, salt marshes, and tidal flats.
  • Act as buffers between land and sea, reducing storm surge impacts.
  • Vital nurseries for marine and estuarine species.

Peatlands

  • Wetlands dominated by accumulated organic matter (peat).
  • Found in tropical and boreal regions.
  • Store twice as much carbon as all the world's forests combined.

Key Facts & Significance

Wetlands cover approximately 1.2 billion hectares globally and support a wide range of life forms, many of which are endemic or threatened. They are home to over 40% of all plant and animal species, despite their relatively small global footprint. Wetlands also play a crucial role in climate regulation by acting as significant carbon sinks, particularly in peatlands. Economically, they provide benefits estimated at $47 trillion annually through ecosystem services like clean water, fisheries, flood protection, and recreation. Importantly, wetlands help in water filtration, trapping pollutants and sediments, and recharging aquifers that supply clean water to millions of people.

Purpose of Wetland Ecosystems

Wetland ecosystems are vital to environmental sustainability and human survival. They function as natural water filters, purifying freshwater supplies by trapping sediments, nutrients, and pollutants. Their ability to absorb and store excess rainfall makes them essential for flood mitigation and storm protection. Wetlands are hotspots of biodiversity, serving as breeding grounds, shelters, and feeding sites for countless species, including migratory birds, amphibians, and fish. They are also significant carbon reservoirs, especially peatlands, which help combat climate change. In many cultures, wetlands have deep spiritual, cultural, and economic importance, supporting traditional livelihoods and community identity.
